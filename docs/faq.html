This analysis documents associations, not proven causal effects. Father absence correlates with many other disadvantages—poverty, lower maternal education, neighborhood quality—that independently affect adult outcomes. The event-time design provides stronger evidence by comparing post-exit outcomes for children who experienced father departure at a specific age to matched controls, but it is still observational.

The NLSY97 provides detailed retrospective household composition from birth through age 17, linked to adult outcomes measured 20+ years later. The longitudinal design enables tracking from childhood exposure to adult economic outcomes in a way that cross-sectional surveys cannot.

Loading artifact summary... The NLSY97 is unweighted and cohort-specific; national surveys are weighted population estimates. See the Methods page for details on each data source.

The event-time design compares children who experienced father exit at a specific age to continuously-present controls, matched on birth year, sex, and race. Post-exit outcomes (schooling, arrests) are measured 3+ years after the event, reducing concerns about reverse causality.
